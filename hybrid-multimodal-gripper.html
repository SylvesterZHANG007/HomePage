Soft‚ÄìRigid Coupling + Pinch‚ÄìSuction Hybrid Mechanism: Enabling Multimodal Grippers for Universal Grasping

Published: June 20, 2025
Soft Robotics Grasping Multimodal Systems

Research Achievement: This research introduces a Hybrid Multimodal Gripper (HMG) that revolutionizes robotic grasping by combining pinch-suction mechanisms with soft-rigid coupling. Published in IEEE Transactions on Robotics (IF = 9.4), this work demonstrates unprecedented grasping diversity from 0.2g feathers to 10kg dumbbells.

Introduction

Universal grasping ‚Äî the ability to adapt to a wide range of objects and tasks without requiring customized adjustments ‚Äî is essential for robotic grippers. A key to achieving this lies in designing grippers with flexibility and adaptability, allowing robots to handle unknown objects and complex environments as humans do. However, the vast diversity in object weight, fragility, size, and shape poses long-standing challenges to this goal.

Previous studies have primarily adopted two strategies to enhance grasping diversity in robotic grippers: multi-mechanism fusion and soft‚Äìrigid structural coupling. However, current robotic grippers often suffer from conflicts between different mechanisms or structural elements, preventing full utilization of their advantages.

Multi-Mechanism Fusion

Each grasping mechanism has its limitations. For instance, enveloping grippers struggle with flat objects, while suction-based grippers cannot handle porous items. Combining pinch and suction mechanisms ‚Äî inspired by an elephant trunk ‚Äî significantly improves adaptability.

Soft‚Äìrigid structural coupling mimics the human finger's internal bone structure and outer soft tissue (muscles and skin), offering both strength and compliance. Rigid grippers provide high output force and precision but lack adaptability, while soft grippers are highly adaptive but have limited load capacity.

Selective Activation Mechanism (SAM)

The gripper incorporates a Selective Activation Mechanism (SAM), enabling complete decoupling between rigid and soft actuators to harness their respective advantages. Additionally, a compliant adaptive module ‚Äî composed of passive telescopic fingers and a retractable soft suction cup ‚Äî allows seamless switching between pinch and suction modes without additional actuators or active control.

Figure 3: Selective Activation Mechanism (SAM) architecture demonstrating the effective coupling design between soft and rigid components, enabling seamless switching between pinch and suction modes without interference.

Advanced Capabilities

Dynamic Environment Performance

In dynamic environments (4.5 m/s¬≤ acceleration), HMG can robustly grasp irregular objects (e.g., plush toys, phone stands, model landscapes) in various orientations, demonstrating strong robustness and adaptability.

Figure 5: Dynamic environment performance demonstration showing HMG's robust grasping capabilities for irregular objects (plush toys, phone stands, model landscapes) under 4.5 m/s¬≤ acceleration in various orientations.

Closed-Loop Sensing

By integrating flexible pressure sensors at the fingertips and suction inlet, HMG gains perceptive ability during interactions, enabling closed-loop control for precise grasping according to preset instructions.

Figure 6: Closed-loop sensing system integration showing flexible pressure sensors at fingertips and suction inlet, enabling precise feedback control and adaptive grasping force modulation for delicate object manipulation.

Real-World Applications

HMG excels in complex real-world tasks through coordinated multimodal operation:

  • Industrial Applications: Assembly line operations, fruit-sorting pipelines
  • Daily Life Tasks: Sorting books, pouring milk, cooking soup
  • Underwater Operations: Sediment sampling, marine archaeology, debris collection
  • Emergency Response: Rescue operations requiring gentle yet secure grasping

Innovation Impact: By integrating multiple grasping mechanisms and a soft‚Äìrigid coupled structure into one gripper, this research demonstrates ‚Äî for the first time ‚Äî the enormous potential for expanding grasping diversity and offers a simple yet effective design solution for multimodal coordination.

Future Directions

These capabilities highlight HMG's potential across daily life, industrial production, and specialized applications. The principles and mechanisms developed provide valuable references for the future development of high-performance robotic grippers, paving the way for truly universal grasping systems.

Future research directions include scaling the technology for larger industrial applications, improving sensing capabilities for better environmental awareness, and developing machine learning algorithms for adaptive grasping strategy selection.
